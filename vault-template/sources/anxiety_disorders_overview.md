# Anxiety Disorders Overview

Anxiety disorders are the most common mental health conditions worldwide, characterised by excessive fear, worry, and related behavioural disturbances. The DSM-5 groups several distinct conditions under this umbrella.

## Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

GAD is defined by persistent, excessive worry about a number of different activities or events, occurring more days than not for at least six months. The worry is difficult to control and is associated with at least three of the following symptoms in adults: restlessness or feeling keyed up/on edge, fatigue, difficulty concentrating, irritability, muscle tension, and sleep disturbance. The worry is not confined to features of another disorder and causes clinically significant distress or functional impairment.

## Panic Disorder

Panic disorder involves recurrent unexpected panic attacks — abrupt surges of intense fear or discomfort that peak within minutes — accompanied by persistent concern about additional attacks or maladaptive behaviour changes related to the attacks. Physical symptoms during an attack may include palpitations, sweating, trembling, shortness of breath, chest pain, nausea, dizziness, and fear of losing control or dying.

## Social Anxiety Disorder

Social anxiety disorder (social phobia) is characterised by marked fear or anxiety about one or more social situations in which the person is exposed to possible scrutiny by others. The person fears they will act in a way or show anxiety symptoms that will be negatively evaluated. The social situations almost always provoke fear or anxiety, are actively avoided or endured with intense distress, and are out of proportion to the actual threat.

## Evidence-Based Treatments

Cognitive-behavioural therapy (CBT) is the first-line psychological treatment for all anxiety disorders. Exposure-based techniques — systematically confronting feared situations rather than avoiding them — are a core component. Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) and mindfulness-based approaches have also demonstrated efficacy.

Pharmacological options include selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) and serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs) as first-line agents, and benzodiazepines for short-term relief only due to dependency risk.

Combined CBT and pharmacotherapy may offer advantages for moderate-to-severe presentations. Patients should always consult a licensed psychiatrist or psychologist for individualised assessment and treatment planning.
